What Is an Epidural Injection?
An epidural injection, often called an epidural steroid injection (ESI), is a non-surgical treatment used to relieve pain caused by inflamed or compressed spinal nerves.
Doctors commonly recommend epidural injections for:
•	Disc problems (herniated or bulging discs)
•	Spinal canal inflammation (spinal stenosis)
•	Nerve root compression causing sciatica
The injection is delivered into the epidural space, the area surrounding the spinal cord and nerve roots. This space contains small blood vessels and fatty tissue. When nerves in this area become irritated or inflamed, pain can radiate from the lower back into the buttocks and down the legs.

Conditions Treated with Epidural Injections
Epidural injections are most often used to treat pain caused by inflammation or injury to spinal nerves. They are especially helpful for patients experiencing:
•	Herniated or bulging discs – When a disc presses on a nerve root, it can cause leg pain (sciatica).
•	Spinal stenosis – Narrowing of the spinal canal that puts pressure on nerves.
•	Degenerative disc disease – Age-related disc wear that leads to nerve irritation.
•	Sciatica – Radiating pain along the sciatic nerve.
•	Spondylolisthesis – When one vertebra slips over another.
•	Post-surgical spine pain – Persistent pain after spinal surgery.

How Soon Will You Feel Relief?
Pain relief varies from person to person.
•	The local anaesthetic may provide immediate but temporary relief.
•	The steroid typically begins reducing inflammation within 2–5 days.
•	Maximum improvement may occur within 7–10 days.
Some patients benefit from a series of 2–3 injections spaced several weeks apart. Your doctor will advise what is appropriate based on your condition and response.

Limitations and Considerations
While epidural injections are effective for many patients, they are not a permanent cure.
Pain relief may be:
•	Temporary
•	Partial
•	Variable between individuals
Long-term management of spinal conditions often involves a combination of treatments, including:
•	Physical therapy
•	Lifestyle adjustments (weight management, posture correction)
•	Non-opioid pain medications
•	Surgical intervention (if conservative treatments fail)
Epidural injections should be viewed as part of a broader pain management strategy rather than a standalone solution.
